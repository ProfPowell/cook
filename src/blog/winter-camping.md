Winter camping is one of the most rewarding outdoor experiences — empty trails, stunning landscapes, and a quiet that only snow can create. But it demands respect and preparation.

## Gear Essentials

Your summer setup won't cut it. Here's what changes:

### Shelter
A 4-season tent like the **Summit Shelter 3P** is non-negotiable. The steeper walls shed snow, the reinforced poles handle wind loads, and the reduced mesh keeps heat in. Set up with the smallest opening facing away from prevailing wind.

### Sleep System
Layer your insulation:

- **Sleeping pad:** R-value of 5.0 or higher. Use two pads stacked if needed — the ground will steal more heat than the air.
- **Sleeping bag:** Rated 10°F below your expected low temperature. Down is lighter; synthetic still insulates when damp.
- **Liner:** Adds 10–15°F to your bag's rating for minimal weight.

### Clothing
The layer system matters more than ever:

1. **Base layer:** Merino wool or synthetic. Never cotton.
2. **Mid layer:** Fleece or lightweight down.
3. **Shell:** Waterproof, windproof, breathable.
4. **Camp layer:** A heavy puffy jacket for when you stop moving.

## Camp Setup Tips

- **Stamp a platform** with your boots or snowshoes before pitching your tent. Let it set for 30 minutes.
- **Cook in your vestibule**, never inside your tent. Carbon monoxide from stoves is lethal in enclosed spaces.
- **Keep water bottles inverted** so ice forms at the bottom, not the cap.
- **Sleep with your boots** in your sleeping bag. Cold boots in the morning are miserable and dangerous.

## The Most Common Mistake

New winter campers overdress at night and wake up sweaty. Moisture in your bag destroys insulation. Start cool — you'll warm up. If you're comfortable when you first get in, you're wearing too much.

---

Start with a night that's expected to be just below freezing. Build your comfort and skills gradually. The mountains will wait.
